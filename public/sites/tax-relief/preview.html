Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about OIC and tax resolution:

What is an Offer in Compromise?

An Offer in Compromise (OIC) is a formal IRS program that allows taxpayers to settle their federal tax debt for less than the full amount owed. It's available when there's genuine financial hardship and the offer reflects what the IRS could reasonably collect.

How long does the OIC process take?

Typically 6–24 months from submission to acceptance or rejection. The timeline depends on complexity, IRS processing capacity, and whether the IRS requests additional documentation. During this period, collection activities generally remain paused.

What happens if my OIC is rejected?

If rejected, you have appeal rights and can pursue alternative strategies: request reconsideration, negotiate an installment agreement, or explore other resolution options like Currently Not Collectible status. We'll guide you through these alternatives.

Do I need a tax attorney?

While not required, legal representation significantly increases OIC acceptance rates. An attorney protects your rights, manages IRS communication, and ensures proper documentation and strategy. This is especially important for complex cases or when significant settlement amounts are involved.

What if I owe state taxes too?

This site focuses on federal OIC. State tax resolution is separate and may involve different programs. We can discuss multi-jurisdiction strategies during your consultation to address both federal and state tax liabilities holistically.

Will OIC affect my credit?

Tax liens and levies already impact credit. An accepted OIC removes the enforcement threat and provides closure, which often improves financial stability and future creditworthiness compared to ongoing collections activity.
