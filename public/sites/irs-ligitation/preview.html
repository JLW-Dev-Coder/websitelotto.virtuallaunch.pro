From Filing to Resolution

Assessment & Notice

IRS issues Statutory Notice of Deficiency. 90-day window opens for Tax Court filing.

Petition Filed

Formal petition filed in Tax Court, District Court, or Claims Court depending on strategy.

Discovery Phase

Exchange of documents, expert reports, and deposition of key witnesses (6-9 months).

Motions & Settlement

Summary judgment motions, settlement conferences, and mediation opportunities.

Trial Preparation

Final witness prep, trial exhibits, and opening statement strategy finalized.

Trial & Resolution

Courtroom argument, judicial opinion issued, post-trial motions if necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I file in Tax Court vs. District Court?

+

Tax Court is ideal for disputing assessments before payment. District Court requires payment first but offers jury trial options and may be better for certain factual disputes. We assess your specific situation to recommend the optimal forum.

What are my chances in court?

+

Litigation outcomes depend on facts, law, judge, and expert evidence. We provide candid assessments after evaluating IRS arguments, comparable cases, and your position strength. Settlement is often the optimal path.

How long does tax litigation take?

+

Tax Court cases typically take 2-4 years from petition to resolution. District Court cases may take 3-5 years. Complex cases with multiple issues or appeals can extend timelines significantly.

Can I settle after litigation begins?

+

Yes. Settlement is possible at any stage—pre-trial, post-discovery, even post-trial before appeals. As litigation progresses and positions clarify, settlement often becomes more attractive to both parties.

What if I disagree with the court decision?

+

Appeals are available to the Circuit Court of Appeals, and in rare cases, to the Supreme Court. We evaluate appealability and appeal strategy after the trial decision. Most cases are not appealed due to cost and likelihood of reversal.
